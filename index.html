Leonhard Euler (1707 - 1783) introduced the $\zeta (s)$ function for real values s, defined as \begin{equation*} \zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s} \end{equation*} He found that it converges for s >1. He also found the value for s=2, known as the Basel problem \begin{equation*} \zeta(2)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6} \end{equation*} and for all the positive even numbers: \begin{equation*} \zeta (2n)={\frac {(-1)^{n+1}B_{2n}(2\pi )^{2n}}{2(2n)!}} \end{equation*} where $B_{2n}$ is the 2n-th Bernoulli number.

In 1937 he also discovered an important relation between $\zeta(s)$ and the prime numbers, the so-called Euler product formula: \begin{equation*} \zeta(s)=\prod_{p}\frac{1}{(1-p^{-s})} \quad for \quad s > 1 \end{equation*} See proof here

Bernhard Riemann introduced radically new ideas and new techniques to study number theory in an analytic way in a paper that he presented to the Berlin Academy of Sciences in 1859. He generalized the $\zeta(s)$ function to complex numbers $s$ \begin{equation*} \zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s} \quad for \quad Re(s) > 1 \end{equation*} and derived its analytic continuation to the whole complex plane through the functional equation \begin{align*}\zeta(s) &= 2^{s} \pi^{s-1} \Gamma(1-s)\sin(\frac{\pi s}{2})\zeta(1-s).\end{align*} where $\Gamma(s)$ is the gamma function. See proof here.



He also derived an exact formula for the number of primes less than x by means of the zeros of the zeta function and put forward the conjecture that all the non-trivial zeroes of the $\zeta$ function lie along the line x = 1/2, this so-called Riemann hypothesis has resisted proof for more than 164 years and constitutes one of the most important open problems in pure mathematics.

The Riemann hypothesis is one of the seven millennium Prize Problems established by the Clay Mathematics Institute.
